Problem Solving

3 Feb 2023 | Sprint 4 | Tech

Describe a time when you were blocked on a problem...

What was the problem?

I'm learning that there is a stark difference between what I think is a problem and what is actually a problem. Often I find myself making problems where there aren't any and causing myself grief. I seem to find myself blocked daily when solving the exercises in this course and because I know that I tend to make problems where there aren't any I can spend too long trying to figure out what problem I have no doubt made up. I don't seem to be able to grasp what the questions are asking of me and I tend to over analyse the solutions. This week I was stuck for a good amount of time trying to make a thing do a thing in a way that wasn't asked because I was thinking ahead and putting myself in real world situations when the exercise was to just do-the-thing...

What problem-solving techniques did you use?

The majority of the time when I'm stuck with code I will rearrange all the wording and see if altering things slightly makes a difference. This is usually where I've gone wrong so I think maybe trying to be more aware of when I'm doing that for too long instead of moving on to a different approach might save me some time and energy.

How did you feel throughout the process?

I absolutely love solving puzzles so I don't particularly dislike being stuck on something as having my brain stay busy is a relief from all the usual crap thats in there.

What did you learn?

From this experience I learned that I am stubborn - I need to accept that there is a time and a place to keep going on my own to try and fix something, and a fast paced course where there is definitely not unlimited time is not it!

Describe a time when you elegantly solved a problem

What was the problem?

There isn't really anything elegant about me, as my dear nan would tell me. I don't have the best memory recall either so trying to think of a time I did anything is hard...

The only thing that is coming to mind is when I created a dynamic 'sales-team-proof' spreadsheet in my last job to help clients provide us with data in the right format to import to our app. It was pretty snazzy, although very niche.

What problem-solving techniques did you use?

I had help from two people in my team that were more proficient in Excel than me, and I used Google to find certain formulas.

How did you feel throughout the process?

It was awesome working with my teammates on the same problem, as we all had a hard time with the process before we had the spreadsheet so when that worked we all felt pretty pleased.

What did you learn?

I learned that you can't possibly know everything, but attempting to learn something makes you more helpful and less of a burden to others. I also learned that there are some people that make money from purposely not knowing anything...

Reflect on your confidence in using the following problem solving methods...

Pseudocode

Making diagrams and explaining each section line by line is how I roll. I need to understand what every line is doing before I can fully grasp the concept. I've been trying to include more comments in my attempts but theres a fine line between making comments to help you figure something out, and overwhelming your eyeballs with text. I haven't quite figured that line out yet.

Trying something

My default approach to life in general is to throw excrement and see what sticks. I didn't realise this was an actual method! Glad to hear it though.

Rubber ducky method

I'm pretty sure I talk to myself as I'm going so that kind of counts. I think my rubby ducky is a pen pal ducky though as I can't seem to get the words out to explain with my face. I find it much easier to type out my issue, as then I actually understand what I'm saying. The words tend to come out slower and more considered.

Reading error messages

I kind of know what some of the things are saying, but when they are huge messages with loads of text and no red lines I get overwhelmed. In my last job we used Sentry, the error logging application which I was very scared of in the beginning. Then once someone explained to me that I only really needed to look at a couple of lines to have it help me I loved it.

Console.logging

Console logging for me goes hand in hand with the trying something method. Every time I make a small change in the code I log it to the console to see what happened. I'm pretty confident that this works quite well in most cases, although not always. I can't always determine what the console is saying, but I'm sure it'll get better with more practice.

Googling

I don't think I'd be alive without Google. When I lived in Indonesia google was my doctor. I'm pretty sure I broke my foot at one point, jumping into a pool whilst slightly intoxicated. Four years later I seem to be recovering well all thanks to Auntie Google, no thanks to my friend who suggested I get a massage to fix it...

I had no idea how many very specific questions were out there on forums for javascript, but I'm so grateful for them! I've managed to find the majority of the things I was looking for with minimal effort.

Asking your peers for help

When I started my last job on day 1 of the first lockdown I quickly became acutely aware that if you want to make new friends in a high stress environment there is a quota of questions you can ask your peers per day.

I try to make sure I can gauge the situation before jumping head first in to annoying mode so I won't always go to my peers first especially if I know they are also struggling as I often find myself confusing people more when I try to help do something I'm not confident in already.

Asking coaches for help

I'm not as hesitant when it comes to coaches though as I know they will be able to point me in the right direction without a second thought.

Improving your process with reflection

In my life long quest for efficiency I'm always reflecting on my thoughts, choices, decisions, attitudes, beliefs. I get great satisfaction from fine tuning life in general, and most people don't like it when I do it to them so I'll continue being my own best and worst critic!
